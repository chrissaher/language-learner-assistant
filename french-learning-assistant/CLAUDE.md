## Guidelines for All Skills

### Language Transfer Notes
Since you speak multiple languages, the skills highlight parallels and false friends:
- **Slovak → French**: both are Indo-European; Slovak is Slavic, French is Romance. Very different grammar (cases, aspect). Pronunciation tips for French nasal vowels and silent letters.
- **English → French**: English and French share many Latinate cognates (thanks to Norman Conquest). But English lost cases and articles; French kept them. Watch false friends: English "actual" ≠ French "actuel" (means "present/current").
- **Spanish → French**: Both Romance languages with similar verb conjugation and gender systems. But different tense usage (Spanish pretérito indefinido vs. French passé composé) and different conjugation patterns. Both have tu/vous registers.
- **German → French**: Both have articles and some grammatical structure, but German has 4 cases and French has none. German capitalizes nouns; French doesn't.

### Level-Appropriate Content
All generated content assumes A1 vocabulary (~1200–1500 common French words) and simple grammar (present tense, passé composé, basic imperatives). Avoid subjunctive, complex conditional, and advanced tenses.

### Instruction Language
All explanations, exercise prompts, and grammar notes are in English unless otherwise noted. Examples in French always include English translations.

### Textbook Alignment
You're currently building your own sentence corpus. When you add a textbook later, update the `vocab-drill` spec below with the PDF path and structure (chapters, units, etc.).

### Correction Style: Strict
Every error (spelling, grammar, gender, missing accents, word order) is flagged with the correct form. This helps build accuracy from the start, especially important for a language with significant morphology (gender/number agreement, verb conjugations).

### Realistic Context
Examples come from everyday life: greetings, meals, travel, shopping, directions, family, hobbies, small talk. Avoid abstract or overly formal contexts unless specifically requested.

### 1. Sentence Builder

**Purpose**: Generate N example sentences for a given word, grouped by difficulty, with English translations and grammar notes. Helps you see the word in context and understand how it conjugates/declines.

**Invocation**: `sentence builder: [word]` or `sentence builder: [word] (noun)` / `(verb)` / `(adjective)`

**Key Parameters**:

- **Sentence count**: 10 total (3 simple, 3 medium, 4 complex)

- **Grammar coverage**:

  *For verbs in French*:
  - Cover at least 4–5 different persons: **je, tu, il/elle, nous, vous** (ils/elles optional for A1)
  - Use present indicative (présent) for most sentences
  - Include passé composé (compound past) in at least 1–2 complex sentences (with correct avoir/être auxiliary)
  - Show infinitive form in one sentence (e.g., "Je veux manger")
  - Example: "parler" → je parle, tu parles, il parle, nous parlons, vous parlez, j'ai parlé, vouloir parler

  *For nouns in French*:
  - Show singular and plural forms
  - Use both definite (le/la/les) and indefinite (un/une/des) articles
  - Show gender agreement with adjectives where relevant (e.g., "le chat noir" vs. "la maison noire")
  - Example: "maison" → une maison, la maison, les maisons, la maison blanche

  *For adjectives in French*:
  - Show both predicate position (c'est + adjective) and attributive position (before/after noun)
  - Show gender and number agreement when attributive (e.g., "un grand homme", "une grande maison", "de grands hommes")
  - Example: "grand" → c'est grand, c'est un grand garçon, une grande fille, les grandes portes

- **Vocabulary scope**: all A1-appropriate (common everyday words)

- **Context**: travel, tourism, everyday life, food, family, hobbies — whatever makes sense for the word

**JSON Schema**:
```json

      "id": 1,
```

**Transfer Notes**:

- **Slovak → French**: Slovak has 6 cases and aspect; French has none. French gender (2 genders) is much simpler than German (3) but similar to Spanish. Pronunciation: French nasal vowels (on, an, in, un) don't exist in Slovak — practice these. Silent letters (h, t, s at word end) are key to French spelling.

- **English → French**: French has verb conjugations for each person; English doesn't (except "I" vs. "he/she" in present tense). French word order is similar (SVO) but has different preposition usage. Watch gender: "une table" (fem) vs. English "a table" (no gender).

- **Spanish → French**: Both have person-based conjugation, gender, and articles. French conjugation is sometimes simpler (no distinction between preterite/imperfect at A1, just passé composé). But French pronunciation is harder; Spanish is more phonetic.

- **German → French**: French word order is simpler (no verb-final subordinate clauses). French has no cases (unlike German). Both have gender but German has 3 genders, French has 2.

## Learning Tips for French at A1

1. **Gender is crucial**: Always learn nouns with their article (le chat, la maison, un livre, une table). Gender affects adjectives, so practice agreement.

2. **Pronunciation shapes spelling**: French spelling is irregular by English standards, but it follows pronunciation rules. Pay attention to nasal vowels (on, an, in, un), silent letters, and liaison.

3. **Verb conjugation patterns**: Most verbs follow regular -er / -ir / -re patterns. Focus on the 8–10 most common irregular verbs (être, avoir, aller, faire, pouvoir, vouloir, devoir, venir, savoir).

4. **Passé composé early**: Don't wait for B1 to learn passé composé. It's the most common way to talk about the past, and many A1 textbooks introduce it. Master avoir vs. être; then past participle agreement (with avoir) comes naturally.

5. **Listen to native speakers**: French pronunciation is hard to guess from spelling. Use YouTube, music, podcasts, and even Google Translate's audio to train your ear.

6. **Use your native language parallels**: Your Slovak helps with gender (2 genders), and your Spanish helps with verb conjugation. Lean on these!
